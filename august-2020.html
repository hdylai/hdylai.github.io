Activity log

On moving remotely

Moving is so anxiety inducing, coordinating one remotely is even more so. Every time I dared to open a window and tackle part of the planning (looking into movers, storage units, size of storage units, types of locks??, etc.), 3 hours of time was guaranteed to be sucked away. Thanks to Esther, our belongings in NYC were successfully moved into a storage unit in Brooklyn, where they'll be until we (hopefully, God-willing) move back after to the city after the holidays. I miss the city, I miss being in close physical proximity to my friends, I miss seeing my coworkers at 1WTC.


On a book I gushed over

I had to restrain myself from finishing Alexander Chee's How to Write an Autobiographical Novel in less than three sittings and adored it. It was a book which truly reminded me why I enjoy reading, & how powerful good writing is to allow readers to take in and steward the weight of someone one else's lived experiences. There's a posture of humility that is continuously prevalent; Chee is certainly a very accomplished writer, his achievements are duly noted throughout his essays. However, there's a sense of grace even in these pieces centered on his life & his experiences that I find quite rare, a self-awareness that lends itself to sincerity rather than egotism. If this wasn't obvious already, he is my new literary crush.


On about me's

Recently, inspired by the book and recent conversations with Sarah & SHK about portfolio revivals, I've been thinking about the futility of writing & creating "about me" pages. As noted in a hastily scrawled journal entry, how do we manifest an online identity without it becoming an artifact that merely upholds the measly status quo of designers to 'have a voice?'

In conversations with friends, it's evident that we life such fruitful lives outside of our work; we have hobbies that keep us excited, friends to keep us grounded and supported. And yet, in our little corners of the internet, we conform to such linear formulas to represent ourselves — this is our present, this is our past, this is a line to demonstrate that we have a life outside of work. Why do we diminish ourselves so? I half-jokingly texted Sarah in the midst of our shared futility, perhaps the key to our anxiety may be to simply create a bot / script to scrape through our social media profiles and write a profile for us with information that's already out in the virtual space — "Hilary is a designer; on this day one year ago, she was hanging out with her friends Soonho, Julia, and Eric, accompanied by Hot Cheetos and the last of their supply of beer from the summer (source: Instagram)."

It feels especially compelling in these uncertain times to change the paradigm of the supposed gravity of these public self-reflections, to reflect in the ways we trade-off between our physical and digital presences, and put more effort into reclaiming some breathing room & not taking ourselves too seriously.


Good documentation

Kris Sowersby's post on his new work, Signifier, for Klim Type Foundry was floating around Twitter this past month, and it didn't disappoint. I don't think I've come across any design documentation in a long time that is so thoughtful and well-considered, especially in the context of translating between physical & digital materiality, and what staying true to the essence of the original form, a punch cutter in this context, looks like. Seeing documentation like this always makes the final output more brilliant & beautiful to me.

One of my favorite quotes from the piece:

My question now became, “what is the raw quality of digital fonts?” Digital fonts are strings of co-ordinates rasterized as images on screens and surfaces. Bézier curves are visualised equations. When we manipulate Bézier handles, we change the parameters of the equation. I stopped working against what I was seeing and started to work with the tools and screen. I deleted details, replaced curves with lines and snapped to the grid. Consciously making things consistent, I practiced a sort of typographic quantization.
